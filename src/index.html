what do proteins have to do with computers?

what is protein folding?

Proteins are the basis for cellular function. But to do their work, they must assemble themselves into a specific shape, or ‘fold’.

Proteins are tiny chains of amino acids that perform every function in the body. Each protein has a specific function when folded, be it . The problem being: we don’t know why they fold in specific ways, making it nearly impossible to tell how a specific protein does its job.

why does folding matter?

Terminal diseases, like Alzheimer’s, Cancer, and Cystic Fibrosis are believed to be caused by protein misfolds.

When we discover how, and why proteins fold into specific structures, we can understand what makes things go wrong. Helping us develop therapies for previously incurable diseases.

Courtesy: Harvard.edu

why do we need computers?

To accurately understand how a protein will fold, we must simulate the forces in the human body every millionth of a second.

With such extreme time scales, and a need for near perfect simulation of conditions, supercomputers are needed to crunch numbers and give us accurate assessments of how a protein may fold.

Just how powerful do computers need to be?

Folding@Home, a distributed computing project, amassed over 1.2 million users during COVID-19. Together, all of these machines were faster than the world's nine fastest supercomputers. Combined.

X-Ray Crystallography, a more standard method of computing protein folds, can take up to 12 months and cost upwards of $125,000 US. These costs often fall back onto consumers so drug companies can recoup their investment.

helping reduce costs

By quickly producing a filtered list of viable protein solutions, scientists can use more expensive methods on more likely candidates.

Distributed computing projects produce open source data. Anything it returns is free for all to use. With free, first-round data available, scientists can get into the complex work of developing drugs with much less upfront cost.
