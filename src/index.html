what do proteins have to do with computers?

what is protein folding?

Proteins are the basis for cellular function. But to do their work, they must assemble themselves into a specific shape, or ‘fold’.

Proteins are tiny chains of molecules that, at a micro level, perform every function in the body. Each protein has a specific function when folded. The problem being: we don’t know why they fold in specific ways, making it nearly impossible to tell what a specific protein does, or how it does it.

Terminal diseases, like Alzheimer’s, cancer, and cystic fibrosis are believed to be caused by protein misfolds.

Courtesy: Harvard.edu

why does folding matter?

When we discover how, and why proteins fold into specific structures, we can understand what makes things go wrong. Helping us develop therapies for previously incurable diseases.

why do we need computers?

Proteins with only 20 amino acids have 10130equally possible folded formations.

With an unfathomable amount of possibilities, simulating protein folds can take months with even very powerful supercomputers.

Through a network of home computers, scientists can boost their folding speeds by hundreds of times.

10000000000000000000000000

Folding@Home amassed over 1.2 million users during COVID-19. Together, all of these machines were faster than the world's nine fastest supercomputers. Combined.

These 1.2 million users were the first to cross the exascale barrier, 1018 operations per second.

helping reduce costs

Distributed computing projects produce open source data. Anything it returns is required to be free for all to use. With free, first-round data available, scientists can get into the complex work of developing drugs with much less upfront cost.
