Distributed computing has helped us understand how proteins fold and pave the way for patent-free drug discovery. Millions of home computers contribute to developing drugs to fight Cancer, COVID-19, and Alzheimer's every day.

what do proteins have to do with computers?

what is protein folding?

Proteins are functional collections of molecules. To do their work, they assemble themselves into a specific shape, or ‘fold’.

Proteins are tiny chains of amino acids that perform every function in the body. The problem being; we don’t know why they fold in specific ways, making it nearly impossible to tell how a specific protein does its job.

why does folding matter?

Terminal diseases, like Alzheimer’s, Cancer, and Cystic Fibrosis are believed to be caused by protein misfolds.

When we discover how, and why proteins fold into specific structures, we can understand what makes things go wrong. Helping us develop therapies for previously incurable diseases.

why do we need computers?

To accurately understand how a protein will fold, we must simulate the forces in the human body every millionth of a second.

With such extreme time scales, each protein chain having more than 1030 different combinations. Supercomputers are needed to even stand a chance at solving for the correct combination.

Just how powerful do computers need to be?

As COVID-19 spread and worldwide isolation came into effect, people wanted to do their part to help fight the pandemic. Folding@Home, a distributed computing project.

Less than two months after Folding @ Home announced participation in COVID-19 antiviral research, over 1,000,000 people were actively donating computing power.

With over 1,000,000 users in 2021, Folding @ Home became more powerful than the worlds ten fastest supercomputers. Combined.

helping reduce costs

The data generated by these computers is public information. Making research costs for other institutions less expensive.

Distributed computing projects produce open source data. Anything it returns is free for all to use. With free, first-round data available, scientists can get into the complex work of developing drugs with much less upfront cost.
